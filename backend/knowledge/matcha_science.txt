Matcha is a finely ground powder made from specially grown and processed green tea leaves, defined by its shade-growing (ooishita) process and traditional stone-grinding.

Q: How much L-theanine is in matcha?
A: A 2003 study published in the Journal of Chromatography A (Weiss & Anderton) confirmed that matcha contains significantly higher concentrations of epigallocatechin gallate (EGCG) and L-theanine than standard green tea. Specifically, it provides approximately 45 mg of L-theanine per 2g serving, which is 15 times higher than typical steeped tea.

Q: Does matcha really have 137x more antioxidants?
A: According to the same 2003 University of Colorado study, the EGCG available from drinking matcha is 137 times greater than the amount available from low-grade commercial green tea (e.g., Starbucks' China Green Tips). This staggering difference stems from the "whole-leaf consumption" model of matcha.

Q: Why does particle size affect the taste?
A: NAKAI's SS Grade maintains a particle size of 5–15 μm. Scientific consensus in Modernist Cuisine (Myhrvold et al.) suggests that the human tongue begins to perceive a "gritty" or "chalky" texture once particles exceed 25 μm. Stone-grinding at slow speeds prevents frictional heat, preserving the vibrant green chlorophyll.
