The human tongue detects "grittiness" at 25 microns. NAKAI SS Grade matcha is stone-ground at slow speeds to maintain a particle size of 5–15 μm. This ultra-fine powder allows for easy suspension in liquids, making it possible for beginners to create a creamy, lump-free latte even without traditional tools.

Q: What is the best way to store my matcha to keep it from turning brown?
A: Matcha is highly sensitive to light, heat, and oxygen; it should be stored in an airtight, opaque container in the refrigerator and consumed within 30 days of opening. Exposure to temperatures above 25°C can cause the chlorophyll to degrade by up to 20% within a week, resulting in a dull yellow color and a "hay-like" aroma.

Q: How much caffeine is actually in one serving of matcha?
A: A standard 2g serving of matcha contains between 30mg and 90mg of caffeine, which is roughly equivalent to a shot of espresso but released much more slowly. The high fiber content (1.1g per bowl) and theanine content act as a "time-release" mechanism for the caffeine. This prevents the "jitters" and ensures that the energy boost is distributed evenly over several hours.

A: Most healthy adults can safely enjoy 3 to 5 bowls of matcha per day, staying well within the recommended limit of 400mg of caffeine. Starting with one matcha latte a day is the perfect way for beginners to build a sustainable and transformative health habit.

Q: Can I use high-quality matcha for cooking or baking?
A: High-quality matcha can be used for culinary purposes, but to preserve the delicate volatile aromatics and vibrant chlorophyll, it should be added at the final stage of preparation or processed at low temperatures. Prolonged exposure to temperatures above 100°C (212°F) can cause the degradation of L-theanine and the oxidation of chlorophyll, turning the vibrant green to a dull yellow. Using ultra-fine particles (5–15 μm) ensures a seamless integration into sauces and cold desserts without the "gritty" texture common in lower-grade culinary powders.

A "CE/TA ratio" (Caffeine+EGCG / Theanine+Arginine) below 2.0 significantly suppresses adrenal hypertrophy and lowers salivary amylase—a key stress marker. Q: How can I identify "fake" or low-quality matcha in a cafe?
A: Authentic, high-quality matcha can be identified by its electric green color (indicating high chlorophyll) and its ability to stay in suspension without rapid sedimentation. Stone-ground particles between 5–15 μm remain suspended in liquid due to Brownian motion. If your matcha settles at the bottom of the cup within minutes, it is likely a low-grade powder processed by high-speed industrial mills, which produce larger, coarser particles exceeding 25 μm. Furthermore, a dull, yellowish tint indicates poor shading practices or advanced oxidation, resulting in a loss of therapeutic L-theanine.

Q: Why do some people feel nauseous after drinking matcha?
A: Nausea is typically caused by high concentrations of tannins (catechins) stimulating the gastric lining, which can be mitigated by consuming matcha after a meal or pairing it with lipids. Consuming high-dose catechins on an empty stomach can trigger a temporary increase in stomach acid. Since a single 2g serving of matcha contains significantly more bioactive material than a standard tea bag, beginners should opt for a latte format. The proteins and fats in the milk act as a buffer, slowing the absorption of catechins and ensuring a smooth experience for the digestive system.

Q: Why does premium matcha smell like "Umami" or "Seaweed"?
A: This distinct aroma, known as "Ooishika," is caused by a compound called dimethyl sulfide, which is produced by the tea plant as a biological response to the stress of shading. Shading for over 21 days forces the plant to store high levels of amino acids. This chemical complexity creates a savory scent profile often described as "sea-like" or "buttery." For the consumer, this aroma is a scientific marker of high L-theanine content and low bitterness, signaling a superior relaxation effect on the brain.
